Read Matt Stuart's book: Think like a Street Photographer

I read the book one evening during the week of the poetic photography exploration. I made a few poetic images (link), but I did not follow the entire exploration. For me, poetic images are ordinary scenes where the color, or quality of light feels sublime. I'm always trying to make poetic images (see my own Instagram as reference). It's something I picked up while studying visual storytelling at Syracuse University.

I read Matt's book because I've always struggled to make pictures in a place with strangers; I worry about how I'm perceived or about being “in the way”. Reading the book reminded me of that past fear, but it also helped me see how I've grown since I started making pictures. I'm more confident approaching and engaging people than I was before. I can assert myself better when making pictures, and while I still don't photograph strangers much, I'm working on it!

Overall I enjoy my own approach to photography and while I'm trying to grow, I appreciate the work I make and how it makes me feel good and in the flow state.

Prepare PhD application materials: cover letter, portfolio

As a parallel track to finishing my DfI master's, I've been applying to PhD positions. In preparing my CV, motivation letter, and portfolio for applications, I got review and revise my journey from engineering to studying design here in Delft.

  • I started by studying engineering at MIT. I learned how to analytically break down situations and problems into chunks I can solve.
  • I chose to study photography because it was a hobby I enjoyed, and I thought there was something special about documenting the world and the variety of stories. While I may have gone in wanting to be a photojournalist, I left interested in doing more than documentation. People's stories are important to tell, but they're not where I wanted to stop to create some tangible change. Also, making a career as a photographer is hard.
  • While getting my M.Sc. in Visual Storytelling, I discovered data journalism and data visualization as a way where I could mix my technical knowledge with storytelling. I dove into that for a while and taught courses at the University of Miami for 2 years before coming to Delft.
  • I chose Design for Interaction at TU Delft because it seemed to be the most flexible master and an environment where I could thrive. I have many skills, and I can move between prototyping and interacting with people nicely.

Watch: André and his Olive Tree (2020)

The movie follows André Chiang as he closes his restaurant André in Singapore. It's a reflection on his career and his ideals. I admire chefs and cooking documentaries because they seem to highlight a similarity between cooking and designing — making something for others at a high level and working with different situations/ingredients. Towards the end of the film there were some moments that resonated with me:

  1. While visiting a night market, a passerby and fan wonders why he returned to Taiwan given that the market for fine dining is not very good. André's saw it differently: “I think it's a mission for me. If everyone believes that the environment won't improve, then it will never improve… I think it's up to us to create that environment and not expect that the environment would be right for us.” This active approach resonated with me as I've been working on becoming more independent as a designer.
  2. André is seen in his Taipei restaurant, RAW. He's giving feedback at the end of the briefing. He's pushing forth the point that it's important to review the wine list every day, even if there are no changes from the day before. It's a form of practice. The briefings are not a place to talk about the good things, but to review the previous day and see what could be improved. Afterwards there's a clip of him giving slightly firmer feedback to an employee, likely a manager. They have to “step up” and remind others to adjust and find ways to improve. Reviewing the clip, it doesn't feel eventful or grandiose, but it's a reminder that keeping a routine and also being critical and looking for ways to improve are both important to growing. By reinforcing our base we can become better. At least, that's what I took from it.

Watch: Rams (2018)

Dieter Rams is the first industrial designer I learned about as an engineer. The principles he proposes for good design are indeed a useful and while I may not find them all applicable to the kind of design work I do, I am deeply intrigued by the coherence and consistency of the products he touched at Braun and Vitsoe. At home in the states, I have a collection of all of books about Dieter Rams; I even took 2 years of German in my bachelor's to be able to read the original German texts to better understand how he's portrayed and his own writing.

Rewatching the documentary was also useful because it reminded me of something I really care about, but haven't been able to explore as much during my master: the communication of design. I think it's great that we write these reports for projects and that we explore contexts and come up with interventions and prototype, but I dislike that we don't focus on communicating the work we do. Design is so pervasive yet it's often seen as a tool for beautifying or making things expensive. I think it's really important to be able to explain the work of design to people who don't study it. I admire architecture critics like Alexandra Lange who can write about buildings and what they mean in our environment, or podcasts like 99 Percent Invisible that shine on little parts of the world that we don't often pay attention to. Gary Hustwit himself does this through his documentaries on various kinds of design (Helvetica, Objectified, Urbanized, Rams). I wish I was better at doing this, and I wonder how I can improve on my own. I'm still figuring out how to bring more of the journalism practice into my own design practice.

Attend Dan Lockton's trial lecture on 13 June

I enjoyed the opportunity to attend Dan Lockton's trial lecture this past week because I could reflect on my own time teaching. As a person Dan is knowledgable and engaging, but the lecture felt a bit too abstract for a bachelor's course (the target audience of the trial was set by the hiring committee, I think) and lacked practicality. These ideas are nice and interesting but as a bachelor's student there has to be something the student can latch on to and use in their current level of work.

Put in the same situation, what would I show? When teaching data journalism or web development, I aim to provide many examples for students to draw inspiration from. The variety helps them find an entry point amidst all the different ways of being a journalist or developer. Within this approach, I wonder sometimes if I'm too practical and not inspiring enough.
