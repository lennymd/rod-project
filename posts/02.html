Design criticism

As part of my drive to understand and communicate the dynamics of the world, I'm always interested in learning about the context in which things are made. My photo editing professor, Mike Davis, would always emphasize that a project could be broken down into 3 questions:

  1. What do you want to do?
  2. How do you want to do it?
  3. Why do you want to do it?

The three parts are interrelated, and understanding why gives you clarity on how to do something. I'm interested in learning more about design and the context in which products are made and evolve because it can provide insights into how they become the way they are. If we can understand this, we can be more intentional when we redesign products.

Another benefit of this line of work that I want to explore is that it would give me the tools to be a good critic for my students. If I have a broad design knowledge, I can offer more tailored suggestions to my students that can help their projects grow. At the end of the day, the research work I aim to do should partly be used to augment my teaching ability.

In this area, I'm inspired by the work of people like:

  • Sara Hendren who has this wonderful book on design and disability, What Can a Body Do?
  • Alexandra Lange who writes so beautifully about architecture and design. Her book, The Design of Childood, was insightful and enjoyable way to understand how childhood has evolved from a mostly unnoticed aspect of life to a very commercialized and intense period of development. I especially enjoyed learning about Froebel's gifts and the impact they had on educational development and architecture.
  • Roman Mars and the entire team behind 99% Invisible.
  • Avery Truffleman and the podcast, Nice Try!, that delves into the history and context and situations of different parts of the household and utopias.
  • Jarret Fuller and his podcast, Scratching the Surface. This podcast has been the source of new inspiration and opportunities to learn about the breadth of design criticism.

Human-Agent Interaction

As an engineer, I was interested in robotics, but I was always flummoxed by the many levels of abstraction involved in developing one. Through the PUUE and ITD courses in TU Delft, I've become more interested in exploring less how robots are made technically, and more how we interact with robots or agents. Agents are typically robots, products or systems that have some level of autonomy and can process information. Most agents are powered by some form of artificial intelligence or machine learning and work through processing details from the world and acting based on predictions.

I'm inspired by the work of Nazli Cila and Iohanna Nicenboim and I want to explore how we can use the uncertainty associated with predictions agents make to make interactions with agents more transparent and healthy.
